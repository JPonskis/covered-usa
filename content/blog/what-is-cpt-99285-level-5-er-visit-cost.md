CPT code 99285 is the highest-complexity emergency department billing code that does not involve critical care. It is reserved for visits where the physician documents high-complexity medical decision-making, typically covering life-threatening or potentially life-threatening conditions. In 2026, the median hospital list price for a single 99285 charge runs between $1,700 and $3,200 before insurance adjustments. That one line on your itemized bill can explain much of why an ER visit costs thousands of dollars.

> **Quick Answer:** CPT 99285 is a Level 5 emergency department evaluation and management (E&M) code. It is the most expensive non-critical-care ER code, requiring documented high-complexity medical decision-making. Hospitals frequently bill it for visits that may only qualify as Level 4 (CPT 99284), costing patients hundreds to thousands of dollars more than they legally owe.

## What CPT 99285 Actually Means

CPT stands for Current Procedural Terminology, a standardized code system maintained by the American Medical Association. Every service performed in a hospital or doctor's office gets assigned a CPT code so insurers and Medicare know what to pay.

Emergency department visits use five E&M codes numbered 99281 through 99285, where 99281 is the lowest complexity (a minor complaint with minimal workup) and 99285 is the highest complexity short of critical care. The jump from Level 4 to Level 5 is not cosmetic. On a hospital chargemaster, that difference can add $1,500 to $2,500 to your bill, according to billing specialists cited in emergency department coding reviews.

To bill 99285 legitimately in 2026, the physician must document high-complexity medical decision-making under CMS guidelines. High complexity means at least two of the following three elements reach the highest threshold:

1. **Problems addressed:** One or more chronic illnesses with severe exacerbation, or a condition posing a threat to life or bodily function.
2. **Data reviewed:** Independent review of tests, independent interpretation of results, or discussion of findings with an external physician.
3. **Risk of complications:** Drug therapy requiring intensive monitoring, or decisions involving possible hospitalization, surgery, or social determinants that increase risk.

The most common legitimate examples are chest pain with suspected myocardial infarction, suspected stroke with imaging, sepsis management, major trauma, and acute respiratory failure. A sprained ankle or a urinary tract infection generally does not meet the threshold regardless of how many tests were ordered.

But the chargemaster rate is where billing errors start, and inflating the E&M level is one of the most common ways that number rises without justification.

## Why Do So Many ER Bills Show CPT 99285?

Research published by Trilliant Health found that coding intensity in outpatient settings has shifted measurably upward over the past several years, with Level 5 ER codes being billed at increasing rates. There are two main reasons.

**The test-count method.** Some hospitals use internal policies that automatically assign Level 5 if a patient receives three or more diagnostic tests, regardless of the physician's actual clinical thinking. If you went to the ER for a migraine and the team ran a CT scan, blood work, and a urinalysis to rule out other causes, the system may auto-assign 99285. CMS does not endorse test-count methodology as a standalone basis for level assignment. The correct basis is medical decision-making complexity, not the number of tests ordered.

**Revenue pressure.** Hospitals have an obvious financial incentive to bill at the highest defensible level. Billing teams may default to 99285 when documentation is borderline rather than erring toward 99284. Auditors from the [Office of Inspector General (OIG) at HHS](https://oig.hhs.gov) have consistently flagged this pattern in compliance reviews.

**Documentation ambiguity.** In a busy ER, a physician may see a genuinely complex patient and make complex decisions without fully documenting the MDM elements required to justify the code. A coder filling in the code later may upgrade to 99285 based on the number of tests rather than documented reasoning.

The practical result is that medical billing errors appear on an estimated 49 to 80 percent of hospital bills, and Level 5 upcoding is one of the most common sources, according to billing specialists at Emergency Room Watchdog.

## What Medicare Pays for CPT 99285 in 2026

Medicare does not pay the chargemaster rate. For 2026, CMS sets reimbursement based on the Physician Fee Schedule and the Outpatient Prospective Payment System. The CMS Medicare Physician Fee Schedule lookup tool at [cms.gov](https://www.cms.gov/medicare/physician-fee-schedule/search/overview) allows you to look up the exact allowed amount for 99285 in your geographic area.

As a general benchmark: Medicare typically reimburses ER E&M codes at roughly 20 to 35 percent of what a hospital charges uninsured patients. Private insurers with in-network contracts pay somewhere between Medicare rates and chargemaster rates, typically 150 to 250 percent of Medicare, according to KFF analysis of commercial insurer payment data.

If you are uninsured and receive a bill showing 99285, the chargemaster list price is almost certainly not what the hospital would accept. Most nonprofit hospitals are required by the ACA and IRS rules to offer charity care and financial assistance programs. Your out-of-pocket cost can often be negotiated down significantly before you pay anything.

## How to Tell If Your 99285 Charge Is Legitimate

If you see CPT 99285 on your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) or itemized hospital bill, ask yourself these questions before paying.

**What was your chief complaint?** If you presented with chest pain, stroke symptoms, sepsis, or a major injury, a Level 5 code is plausible. If you went in for a minor infection, a small laceration, or a condition that was treated and discharged in under two hours without intensive intervention, the code warrants scrutiny.

**Were you admitted?** Level 5 visits often but not always result in admission or a specific high-intensity intervention. Same-day discharge with a prescription and discharge instructions is more consistent with Level 3 or Level 4.

**What tests were run?** Three or more tests does not automatically equal Level 5 under proper CMS coding rules. The tests must have been reviewed, interpreted, and factored into a complex treatment decision.

**Does the physician note exist?** You have the right to request your medical records including the treating physician's note. The MDM documentation in that note must support the 99285 level. If it is sparse or does not document the elements required for high complexity, you have grounds to dispute the code.

## How to Dispute a CPT 99285 Charge

Disputing an incorrect Level 5 code is a straightforward process, though it requires persistence. Here are the steps to follow in 2026.

**Step 1: Request your itemized bill.** Call the hospital billing department and ask for a line-by-line itemized statement showing every CPT code billed. This is your legal right under federal law. Do not pay from a summary statement alone.

**Step 2: Request your medical records.** Ask for the treating physician's documentation including the history, examination, and medical decision-making note. Under HIPAA, hospitals must provide these within 30 days.

**Step 3: Ask for the acuity documentation.** Specifically ask the billing department for the "Acuity Sheet" or "Level Justification" used to assign Level 5. This is the internal document that explains why 99285 was chosen over 99284.

**Step 4: File a formal billing dispute.** If the documentation does not support the code, submit a written dispute to the hospital billing department. State the CPT code in question, the date of service, and your reason for dispute. Request a clinical review.

**Step 5: File an insurance appeal if insured.** If you are insured, contact your insurer and ask them to review the coding on your claim. Insurers have coding experts who can assess whether the billed level matches the medical records.

**Step 6: Escalate if necessary.** If the hospital refuses to correct a legitimate error, you can file a complaint with your state insurance commissioner, the [CMS Office of Inspector General](https://oig.hhs.gov), or learn about your rights under the [No Surprises Act](https://www.cms.gov/nosurprises) and consult a patient advocate.

**Documents You Will Need**
- Itemized hospital bill with all CPT codes
- Explanation of Benefits from your insurer
- Treating physician's ER note (request from medical records)
- Any acuity documentation the hospital used to assign the level
- Photo ID and insurance card for verification

**Common Reasons Disputes Are Denied (and How to Respond)**
- "Our coding meets internal standards": request the specific MDM documentation; internal standards must still follow CMS guidelines.
- "The physician ordered many tests": test count alone does not determine E&M level under 2026 CMS rules.
- "The final code was reviewed by a coder": coders assign based on documentation; if the physician note is thin, that is the hospital's documentation problem, not yours.

Hospitals that accept Medicare or Medicaid funding are required to have financial assistance programs under the ACA. ## The Broader Problem With ER Billing in 2026

The Level 5 upcoding issue sits inside a larger structural problem: ER billing complexity is intentionally opaque. Hospitals set chargemaster prices that bear little relationship to actual cost or fair market value. Uninsured patients face the highest prices while insured patients and Medicare beneficiaries pay fractions of those amounts. The difference between a Level 4 and Level 5 code can determine whether a patient faces a $500 bill or a $2,500 bill, and that decision often happens without any patient input.

Federal price transparency rules that took effect in 2021 require hospitals to post their rates online, including negotiated rates with each payer. The [CMS Hospital Price Transparency rule](https://www.cms.gov/medicare/payment/prospective-payment-systems/hospital-outpatient) mandates that hospitals publish machine-readable price files. In practice, many hospitals have been slow to comply or have posted files that are difficult to interpret without specialized software.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does CPT code 99285 mean on my hospital bill?

CPT 99285 is the Level 5 emergency department evaluation and management code. It indicates your visit was billed at the highest complexity level for non-critical ER care. The code appears on your Explanation of Benefits and itemized hospital bill. It requires documentation of high-complexity medical decision-making by the treating physician.

### How much does a 99285 ER visit cost in 2026?

The median hospital list price (chargemaster rate) for CPT 99285 in 2026 is approximately $1,784, with a range of roughly $863 to $3,194 depending on the hospital and geographic area, per PayerPrice fee schedule data. What you actually pay depends on your insurance status. Medicare pays a fraction of the list price. Insured patients pay their cost-sharing portion after the insurer's negotiated discount. Uninsured patients may qualify for charity care.

### Can I dispute a CPT 99285 charge if I think it is wrong?

Yes. You can request your itemized bill, medical records, and the acuity documentation the hospital used to assign Level 5. If the physician's note does not document high-complexity medical decision-making as defined by CMS guidelines, you can file a written billing dispute with the hospital and an appeal with your insurer. Many patients successfully get Level 5 codes reduced to Level 4 when documentation is borderline.

### What is the difference between CPT 99284 and 99285?

CPT 99284 is a Level 4 ER visit requiring moderate-to-high complexity medical decision-making. CPT 99285 is Level 5 requiring high complexity with conditions posing a threat to life or physiologic function. The difference in hospital list price is typically $500 to $1,500. The deciding factor is the physician's documented medical decision-making, not the number of tests ordered.

### Why does my ER bill show 99285 when I only had a minor problem?

This is a common billing issue known as upcoding. Some hospitals use a test-count method that automatically assigns Level 5 when three or more diagnostic tests are ordered, regardless of clinical complexity. CMS guidance does not support using test count as the sole basis for level assignment. If your condition was minor and you were discharged quickly with minimal intervention, you may have grounds to dispute the code.

### Does Medicare cover CPT 99285?

Yes. Medicare Part B covers emergency department visits including Level 5 visits billed under CPT 99285. Medicare pays a set rate determined by the Outpatient Prospective Payment System or the Physician Fee Schedule, which is substantially below the hospital chargemaster price. Your Part B coinsurance is typically 20 percent of the Medicare-approved amount after your deductible. You can look up the exact Medicare payment rate for 99285 in your area using the CMS Physician Fee Schedule lookup tool at cms.gov.
