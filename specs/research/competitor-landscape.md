# CoveredUSA Competitor Landscape

*Research date: May 13, 2026. Bing organic results, sitemap heuristics from `site:` searches, content shape from direct fetches.*

---

## TL;DR

**Top leverage template:** Procedure costs. The category is fragmented across many low-authority "cost guide" sites (BillArmor, BetterCare, Cura4U, Doctor2me) sitting next to GoodRx and SingleCare, none of which combine site-of-service spread + Medicare rate + year anchor + Spanish in one shape. CoveredUSA can win on schema discipline, bilingual coverage, and CMS-rate authority.

**Top 3 competitors to watch:**
1. **GoodRx** — the default citation for drug costs and increasingly procedure costs. Aggressive freshness, strong FAQ schema, PharmD bylines, programmatic at scale. They are the bar.
2. **NerdWallet** — owns "Best Medicare Advantage in [State] 2026." 50 state pages, freshly dated, CMS data, JD Power scores. They will outrank us on state Medicare Advantage queries.
3. **Healthcare.gov / Medicare.gov** — government .gov authority on glossary, trigger events, and Q&A. We cannot outrank them on `does Medicare cover X` head terms.

**Lowest leverage templates:** Glossary (Healthcare.gov + Investopedia + carrier glossaries are unbeatable for head terms) and Q&A head terms (Medicare.gov wins).

## Per-Template Scorecard

### 1) Procedure Costs

**Top results (Bing, "MRI cost without insurance 2026"):** Craft Body Scan, Ezra, BetterCare, IvieScan, RadiologyAssist, BillArmor, GoodRx, Cura4U, SingleCare, Doctor2me. For colonoscopy: GoodRx, CareCredit, ACS, KFF Health News (investigative), ColonoscopyAssist, CostInsightHub, StopColonCancerNow, Denefits, CareCostIndex, AGA PDF.

**Sitemap signal:** GoodRx publishes hundreds of `goodrx.com/health-topic/...cost` and `goodrx.com/conditions/.../cost` pages. SingleCare runs `singlecare.com/blog/[procedure]-cost`. Healthcare Bluebook (Vālenz) is a tool, not SEO content — it ranks weakly because the cost pages live behind a search UI. FAIR Health Consumer is similar: authority brand, tool-driven, weak SEO surface.

**Content shape:** 1,500–3,500 words, "without insurance / with insurance" splits, body-part breakouts, factor lists, save-money sections. GoodRx uses MedicalWebPage + FAQPage schema and PharmD/MD bylines. Year is variable — many "2026" titles, but data sometimes recycled from 2024.

**Spanish:** Almost none. GoodRx has some Spanish but not on procedure cost pages. Wide-open gap.

**Commercial intent:** Cost-aggregator referral funnels (RadiologyAssist, ColonoscopyAssist), drug-coupon funnels (GoodRx, SingleCare), and a long tail of insurance-quote affiliate sites.

**Leverage:** HIGH. The strongest pages (GoodRx, SingleCare) are beatable on (a) Medicare reimbursement rate inclusion, (b) site-of-service spread tables, (c) Spanish, (d) state-specific anchors. The weakest competitors (Cura4U, Doctor2me, BillArmor, BetterCare, CostInsightHub) are AI-spun cost pages with no real authority — we should not lose to them.

### 2) Drug Costs

**Top results ("Ozempic cost 2026"):** Ozempic.com, Drugs.com, Noom, GoodRx (x2 — landing page + savings guide), WeightWatchers, Healthline, TrimRX (x2). Bing serves a heavy commercial layer: Noom + TrimRX are funnel sites, not pure info.

**Sitemap signal:** GoodRx has the deepest drug catalog (effectively every approved RX has a page). Drugs.com is comparable. Healthline has fewer but better-optimized "drug X cost" pages.

**Content shape:** GoodRx pages run 2,000–3,500 words with FAQPage + Drug + MedicalWebPage schema, PharmD bylines, manufacturer pricing, GoodRx coupon CTA, Medicare-coverage subsections, and update dates within 30 days. Healthline runs 2,500–3,000 words with PharmD-written + medically reviewed bylines, FAQPage schema, no coupon CTA but heavy affiliate cross-links. Drugs.com is encyclopedic but thin on storytelling.

**Spanish:** GoodRx has some Spanish. Healthline has Spanish on top topics but spotty on drug-specific cost pages.

**Commercial intent:** Coupon/savings is the universal CTA. Manufacturer site (ozempic.com) ranks #1 for branded queries — we will not displace it.

**Leverage:** MEDIUM-LOW for head drugs (Ozempic, insulin), MEDIUM for second-tier drugs and class-level queries ("GLP-1 cost Medicare 2026", "Part D OOP cap explained"). The Part D $2,100 cap and the Drug Price Negotiation Program updates are timely, news-driven content opportunities where GoodRx and Healthline have not locked down every variant.

### 3) Healthcare Q&A

**Top results ("does Medicare cover dental 2026"):** Medicare.gov, Skyline Benefit, The Big 65, CMS.gov, Center for Medicare Advocacy, US News, Delta Dental WA, UHC Dental, DentalPlans.com, HealthPartners. AARP also ranks well on the broader dental queries.

**Sitemap signal:** Medicare.gov has a tight, finite Q&A surface (`medicare.gov/coverage/[service]`). It's small but authoritative. AARP runs hundreds of `aarp.org/medicare/...` Q&A pages. NCOA and Healthline cover similar territory at scale.

**Content shape:** Government pages are short (300–700 words), no schema beyond breadcrumbs, no FAQ markup, government tone. AARP runs 800–1,500 word Q&A with editor bylines and frequent updates. Carrier sites (Cigna, Humana, UHC) publish similar Q&A as content marketing.

**Spanish:** Medicare.gov has a Spanish toggle (es.medicare.gov). AARP has limited Spanish.

**Commercial intent:** Government = none. Carrier sites = quote/lead. AARP = membership.

**Leverage:** LOW for head queries (`does Medicare cover dental`) — Medicare.gov will own them. MEDIUM-HIGH for long-tail and combo queries: `does Medicare cover dental in 2026 with the new rule`, `does Medicaid cover rehab in Texas`, `does Medicare cover Ozempic for weight loss`. The combo `does [program] cover [thing] in [state] [year]` is wide open.

### 4) Glossary

**Top results ("what is a deductible health insurance"):** Healthcare.gov glossary, UHC, healthinsurance.org, Kaiser Permanente, Anthem, CareFirst, eHealth, Healthline, Aetna, Fidelity.

**Sitemap signal:** Healthcare.gov runs a fixed glossary of ~150 terms. Carriers run parallel glossaries (UHC, Aetna, Cigna, Anthem each have one). Investopedia has the financial-glossary halo but does not dominate health terms. GoodRx has a `goodrx.com/insurance/health-insurance/terminology-glossary` plus standalone deep-dive pages per term.

**Content shape:** Healthcare.gov glossary entries are 100–250 words, no schema, government-plain. Carrier glossaries are similar. GoodRx and Healthline expand each term into a 1,500–2,500 word standalone explainer with FAQ schema.

**Spanish:** Healthcare.gov glossary has Spanish equivalents (CuidadoDeSalud.gov). Carriers are mostly English-only.

**Commercial intent:** Carrier and GoodRx pages funnel to quotes and coupons. Government = none.

**Leverage:** LOW. Head glossary terms are saturated by .gov and high-DR carrier sites. Real money in this template is the 2-hop pages — `out-of-pocket maximum vs deductible 2026`, `coinsurance calculator example`, `MOOP for family plans` — where GoodRx and Healthline have partial coverage but not complete coverage.

### 5) Trigger Events

**Top results ("just lost job health insurance"):** Healthcare.gov, BenAvest, healthinsurance.org, Washington HPF, Brookings, KFF, HealthPlusLife, Anthem, CMS PDF. For "turning 26": Healthcare.gov, RKA Insurance, GoodRx, MoneyGeek, AveraHealth, NAIC, BCBSTX, HealthcareInsider, CoverHealthCA, UHC. For "turning 65": Medicare.gov, MedicareFaq, EBInsurance, Kiplinger, NewMedicare, paulbinsurance, plus a YouTube result.

**Sitemap signal:** Healthcare.gov has tight, canonical pages for each trigger. healthinsurance.org has the deepest editorial coverage (Louise Norris is the bylined expert and her FAQ pages rank well). Boomer Benefits has strong turning-65 coverage. KFF has the policy-analysis layer.

**Content shape:** Healthcare.gov pages are 500–800 words, list-driven, no schema. healthinsurance.org runs 2,000–3,500 words with detailed scenarios. Boomer Benefits hits 2,500+ words with conversational tone and broker CTAs. NerdWallet uses comparison tables.

**Spanish:** Healthcare.gov has Spanish toggle. The rest are English-only with occasional translated landing pages.

**Commercial intent:** Strong. These are highest-intent moments — someone losing coverage is shopping. Lead-gen friendly.

**Leverage:** HIGH. Trigger events are emotional, query-rich, and the .gov pages are skeletal. There's room for a CoveredUSA-shaped page that says "you just lost your job in [state] in [month/year], here are your exact next 60 days." Spanish coverage is weak across the board. State + life-event combos (`turning 26 California 2026`, `lost job Texas 2026`) are wide open.

### 6) Personas

**Top results ("health insurance for gig workers 2026"):** BenAvest, HSAforAmerica (x2), healthinsurance.org, MyWowHealth, BCBSIL, Life143, BenAvest again, GigWise. Catch.co ranks for branded freelancer queries. Stride Health ranks for some 1099-related queries via blog and product pages.

**Sitemap signal:** Stride Health has a thin SEO surface — they rely on app installs and partnerships, not editorial. Catch.co has more SEO content (platform-specific pages: Fiverr, Upwork, Freelancers Union) but still thin. HSAforAmerica has a real blog. BenAvest publishes the most "self-employed" + "gig worker" content. Freelancers Union has some content but mostly community-oriented.

**Content shape:** 2,000–3,500 words, options breakdowns (Marketplace, Medicaid, COBRA, HSA, sharing, short-term), subsidy math, tax-deduction angle. NerdWallet has parallel content with strong tables. Healthline has some.

**Spanish:** Almost none.

**Commercial intent:** Strong — these are buyers shopping for plans. Affiliate-heavy.

**Leverage:** HIGH. The persona space is fragmented, Stride and Catch (the natural app brands) are not winning SEO, and there is room for a CoveredUSA persona page that ties (a) the 2026 subsidy cliff above 400% FPL, (b) state-by-state options, (c) tax-deduction interplay, (d) Spanish coverage. Persona × state is wide open: `health insurance for Uber drivers in California 2026`.

### 7) State Medicare Advantage

**Top results ("best Medicare Advantage plans California 2026"):** NerdWallet, MedicareGuide.com, US News, SCAN (carrier), Medicare.org, Anthem, CMS landscape PDF, Skyline Benefit, DHCS Medi-Cal, MedicareAdvantage.com. Also significant: medicalinsurancetoday.com PPO shake-up coverage; medicareadvantage.com county-level pages.

**Sitemap signal:** NerdWallet has 49 state pages (no Alaska). MedicareGuide.com has 50. US News has 50. eHealth has 50. MedicareAdvantage.com goes to county level (3,000+ pages). Forbes Advisor has the category but the `site:` search returns nothing — possibly noindex on key state pages, possibly Bing index miss.

**Content shape:** NerdWallet ~3,500–4,000 words, JD Power and CMS Star Ratings, comparison tables, Article schema, May 2026 publish dates. MedicareGuide.com ~2,800 words, schema, methodology citations (CMS Part C, JD Power 2025), broker phone CTAs. US News heavy on aggregate ratings, lighter on plan detail. Carrier sites rank for branded queries.

**Spanish:** None of the editorial competitors have Spanish state pages. Medicare.gov has Spanish but not state-by-state plan comparison.

**Commercial intent:** Very high — broker lead-gen.

**Leverage:** MEDIUM. The top 5 slots are owned by NerdWallet / US News / MedicareGuide / eHealth, each with substantial DR. We can probably grab page 1 with disciplined schema + freshness + Spanish + county-level depth, but we will not displace NerdWallet on `best Medicare Advantage California 2026`. Better play: county-level pages and `Medicare Advantage [state] for [persona]` long-tail.

**Mass-produce first:**
1. **Procedure costs** — broad weak field, schema + Spanish + Medicare-rate moats.
2. **Trigger events** — high intent, government pages are thin, state combos open.
3. **Personas** — fragmented competition, Stride and Catch are not winning SEO.

**Mass-produce later:**
4. **State Medicare Advantage** — competitive but doable with discipline; the freshness arms race favors the program-driven publisher.
5. **Drug costs** — pick second-tier drugs and class/policy queries, skip head terms.

**Do not bother (head terms):**
6. **Glossary** head terms — Healthcare.gov + carriers + Investopedia are entrenched.
7. **Q&A** head terms — Medicare.gov + AARP own them.

(Glossary and Q&A still have value as internal-link nodes and long-tail. We just shouldn't measure them on head-term traffic.)

## AI Citation Gap Analysis

Across Perplexity and Copilot answers in this research session, citations clustered around:

- **GoodRx** (procedure + drug cost) — cited heavily, basically the default.
- **Healthcare.gov / Medicare.gov / CMS.gov** — cited for trigger events, glossary, Q&A.
- **KFF / KFF Health News** — cited for policy explainers and journalism.
- **Healthline** — cited for drug cost and trigger events.
- **NerdWallet / MedicareGuide / US News** — cited for state Medicare Advantage.
- **AARP / NCOA** — cited for dental, Medicare Q&A.

**Citation gap (impressions exist, AI does not pick up):**
- Lower-tier cost-guide sites (Cura4U, Doctor2me, BillArmor, CostInsightHub, BetterCare) rank on Bing but rarely get cited. AI is filtering for authority. CoveredUSA needs to look more like GoodRx/KFF than like BillArmor — concretely: bylined author with credential, dated update line, sourced statistics, FAQ schema, internal links to canonical .gov sources.
- Persona category has almost no AI-cited "winner" outside of Healthcare.gov. This is a wide-open citation slot.
- Spanish equivalents are essentially absent from AI citations because the surface barely exists. Building bilingual versions buys us AI citations in Spanish-language answers with low competition.

**AI-citation playbook for CoveredUSA:**
1. Every page: bylined author with stated credential, "updated [date]" line, sources block citing CMS/Medicare.gov/KFF/manufacturer.
2. FAQPage + MedicalWebPage schema on every page (and MedicalProcedure / Drug where applicable).
3. Numeric specificity (dollar figures, year, FPL %s) — AI loves concrete numbers it can quote.
4. Quote-able sentences engineered for AI extraction: short, fact-dense, near the top of the page.

## Are Any Competitors Using a Template-Driven Approach?

Yes. Studying them:

- **NerdWallet** runs a clear state-Medicare-Advantage template (49 states). Same H1 pattern, same comparison-table structure, same author/editor pair, same May 2026 publish wave. Tells: they re-publish the entire set each year — a coordinated freshness drop. **Lesson:** schedule a single annual republish event for every state page, same week, with all 2026 data swapped in.
- **MedicareGuide.com** is the same play with smaller scale. Same Michael LaPick byline across pages. Same methodology block.
- **GoodRx** templates procedure-cost and drug-cost pages aggressively. Same FAQ schema, same PharmD byline pool, same internal link block. **Lesson:** maintain a small pool of bylined "authors" with stated credentials and rotate them; do not generic-bot the bylines.
- **Healthcare Bluebook (Vālenz) and FAIR Health Consumer** are tool-driven and intentionally weak on programmatic SEO — they sell to employers and payers. Their underwhelming SEO surface is one of the reasons procedure costs are leverage-positive for CoveredUSA.
- **Healthcare.gov / Medicare.gov** are template-driven (one page per Q&A, one page per coverage topic, one page per trigger). They under-invest in length, schema, and freshness on the assumption that domain authority carries them. They are right at the head and wrong at the long tail.

## Schema.org Usage Observed

- **GoodRx:** MedicalWebPage, FAQPage, Drug (on drug pages), Article. Consistent.
- **Healthline:** MedicalWebPage, FAQPage, Article, medically-reviewed metadata. Consistent.
- **NerdWallet:** Article, Review/AggregateRating, BreadcrumbList, Organization, Product (for plans).
- **MedicareGuide.com:** Article + Person + Organization. Lighter than NerdWallet.
- **Healthcare.gov / Medicare.gov:** essentially none beyond BreadcrumbList. Big opportunity vs them.
- **KFF, AARP:** Article only.
- **Healthcare Bluebook, FAIR Health:** none on the consumer pages we sampled.

**CoveredUSA schema target:** MedicalWebPage on every page, FAQPage on every page (with 3–7 Qs), MedicalProcedure on procedure pages, Drug on drug pages, Article + Person + Organization base, BreadcrumbList. This puts us at parity with GoodRx and ahead of Healthcare.gov and Medicare.gov for our query space — and AI-citation-eligible.

## Year-Anchored Programmatic State Pages — Who's Doing It Well

- **NerdWallet** is the cleanest example. 49 state pages, all republished May 2026 with current CMS data, same byline pair, same schema. This is the bar.
- **MedicareGuide.com** does it well at smaller scale with explicit "Updated May 8, 2026" timestamps.
- **eHealth** has all 50 states but the pages feel less recently refreshed and lean toward eligibility content over plan comparison.
- **US News** runs the state pages but anchors on "Best [X] Medicare Advantage" rankings rather than program detail.
- **MedicareAdvantage.com** is the volume leader — county-level pages — but the content is thinner and less authoritative per page. They get traffic via sheer surface area.

CoveredUSA's path: NerdWallet's depth, MedicareAdvantage.com's county surface, plus Spanish and persona splits no one is doing.

## Strategic Recommendations

1. **Mass-produce Procedure Costs first.** Target site-of-service spread (hospital vs ambulatory surgery center vs imaging center), Medicare reimbursement rate (CMS fee schedule), and a Spanish twin for every page. The bar is GoodRx — match their schema and byline discipline and we will outrank the long tail of low-DR cost-guide sites immediately.

2. **Build Trigger Events as the conversion engine.** Highest intent, weakest editorial layer, and Healthcare.gov leaves obvious gaps (state selector, 2026 subsidy cliff, COBRA-vs-Marketplace math, Spanish). Each trigger × state × year is a real page worth shipping.

3. **Personas: pick five and ship deep.** Gig workers / rideshare drivers / self-employed / freelancers / part-time. State-split each. Tie taxes + subsidy math + plan comparison in one page. Stride and Catch are not winning SEO — claim the territory.

4. **State Medicare Advantage: ship the 50, but accept #3-#5 ranks.** We won't take #1 from NerdWallet. We can take #3-#5 and dominate long-tail (county pages, dual-eligible pages, Spanish pages).

5. **Skip head-term glossary and head-term Q&A as growth bets.** Keep them as internal-link nodes only. Do not measure them on head-term rank.

6. **Schema + bilingual + bylined-credential are non-negotiable.** Every page needs FAQPage + MedicalWebPage minimum, Spanish twin, named author with stated credential, dated update line, sources block. This is the AI-citation eligibility threshold.

7. **Freshness cadence: one annual coordinated republish for every state and year-anchored page (the NerdWallet play), monthly spot updates for procedure and drug prices when CMS or manufacturers update.**

8. **Run one or two head-term moonshots — but treat them as marketing, not SEO.** Pick `does Medicare cover dental 2026` and `MRI cost without insurance 2026` and write the world's best page on each. Even if we don't rank, those pages become reference assets and AI-citation magnets.
